# Calibration eval — is the routing score trustworthy?

> Chief routes on one number: `score ≥ scene-threshold`. That invites two fair
> questions. **Discrimination:** does a higher score really mean the user is more
> likely to want the interrupt? **Calibration:** does a score *value* mean what it
> says? Both are answerable offline with *real* errors — the cohort's held-out
> stream makes genuine interrupt predictions against each persona's hidden
> preferences, so ~7,000 `(score, wanted)` pairs with real mistakes fall out of it.

```bash
uv run chief eval --calibration      # writes eval/reports/calibration.md
```

## Headline (deterministic — regenerated by the command above)

- **The raw salience score ranks *backwards*.** Before learning, AUC is **0.368**
  — *below* chance. In this population the loud topics are the unwanted ones
  (newsletters, strength 0.70, wanted by 6%) and the wanted ones are quiet
  (incidents, strength 0.44, wanted by 92%). A router that trusted face-value
  salience would be actively wrong.
- **Preference learning inverts it into a strong discriminator: AUC 0.368 →
  0.918.** Taught only by ±1 feedback, the score learns to rank what *this* user
  wants above what merely shouts.
- **The score is well-ordered but not natively a probability.** Empirical
  P(wanted) is monotone in the score; a parameter-free monotone recalibration
  (fit on half the data, scored on the other half) cuts ECE **0.263 → 0.011**.

## Discrimination — AUC (assumption-free)

AUC is pure rank order — the probability that a wanted event outscores an unwanted
one — so it needs no normalization choice. It's the cleanest claim here:

| weights | AUC | reading |
|---|---|---|
| uniform (before) | **0.368** | salience alone ranks *below chance* |
| learned (after) | **0.918** | the score is now a strong interrupt signal |

The +0.55 swing is the whole thesis of Chief's learning loop, stated as one
assumption-free number.

## Reliability — P(wanted) by score bin

Mapping the score to [0,1] by its structural maximum (`5·0.5 = 2.5`), the
empirical fraction of wanted events climbs monotonically with the score:

```
  score bin      P(wanted)              n
  0.1–0.2   |██                  |  10%  (3018)
  0.2–0.3   |██████████          |  51%  (2555)
  0.3–0.4   |████████████████████| 100%  (1477)
  0.4–0.5   |████████████████████| 100%  (106)
  0.5–0.6   |████████████████████| 100%  (44)
```

A well-ordered decision variable never dips as the score rises — this one
doesn't. The gap between the bar height and the naive bin midpoint is the raw
ECE (**0.263**): the *ordering* is right, but the raw number isn't a probability.
A monotone isotonic map — fit on an interleaved half and scored on the held-out
half so it can't memorize — closes that to **0.011**.

## Per-scene operating points

Every scene's interrupt threshold is a deliberate point on this same score axis.
Quieter scenes sit low (favor recall); demanding scenes sit high (favor
precision) — exactly the trade a chief of staff makes for you:

| scene | threshold | precision | recall |
|---|---|---|---|
| idle | 0.45 | 94% | 83% |
| commuting | 0.60 | 100% | 80% |
| social | 0.70 | 98% | 79% |
| deep_work | 0.85 | 100% | 58% |
| meeting | 0.90 | 100% | 51% |

In a meeting Chief interrupts for barely half of what you'd want — and is right
essentially every time it does. Idle, it surfaces 83% and tolerates a little
noise. The thresholds aren't arbitrary constants; they're chosen operating points
on a score the calibration above shows is trustworthy.

## Method & honesty

- **A view over `run_cohort()`** — the same seeded, offline held-out stream, so
  every number is byte-stable and pinned in
  [`tests/test_calibration.py`](../../tests/test_calibration.py).
- **AUC needs no probability assumption.** It is the one number here free of any
  normalization choice, which is why it carries the headline. The reliability/ECE
  figures depend on the disclosed score→[0,1] map (÷2.5); the isotonic step
  *removes* that dependence by learning the map, and is scored on held-out data.
- **Isotonic PAV is measurement, not a new code path.** It exists here to show the
  score is *calibratable*; the shipped router stays plain score-vs-threshold
  (SPEC §13: no heavy ML).

## Why this matters

The ablation proves the architecture is load-bearing; the cohort proves Chief
learns. This proves the single number both of them turn on is *trustworthy* — and
honest about the one place it isn't (the raw score is an ordering, not a
probability, and the report says so and shows the fix). Discrimination,
calibration, and the exact operating point each scene picks: one command, no
prose required to believe it.
